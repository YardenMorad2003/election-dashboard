🧮 The Party System 1992–2022 — Fragmentation, Volatility and Nationalization

Three classic measures across the 13 elections. The effective number of parties (Laakso–Taagepera) measures fragmentation; Pedersen volatility measures what share of the vote switched lists between consecutive elections; the nationalization score (Jones–Mainwaring) measures how uniform a party's share is across the country (1 = perfectly uniform). The combined story: the list level is stormy — record fragmentation and high volatility; the bloc level is far more stable; and parties are becoming increasingly geographic — nationalization declines steadily, consistent with the geographic-sorting finding on the findings page.

Literature anchor: The effective number by seats here reproduces The Elections in Israel series one-for-one — 8.69 in 1999 and 6.17 in 2003; the 2003 volume presents that drop as a product of repealing direct PM election, which returned votes to the large parties without restoring pre-1996 concentration. The party-system book diagnoses the post-2003 period as a non-system with no stable structure at the list level alongside high bloc stability — precisely the gap measured here between list volatility (mean ~29%) and bloc volatility (~12%). And the 2006 peak (41.4% here) is documented in the literature at the individual level too: 63% of voters switched parties while bloc loyalty held.
